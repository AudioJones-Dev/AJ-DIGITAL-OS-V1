## 1. Purpose

The Founder Intelligence Product Factory defines the repeatable AJ Digital methodology for transforming business problems into deployable systems, software products, automations, AI agents, operational intelligence platforms, and Founder Intelligence Systems.

This is doctrine, not a sales narrative and not a step-by-step SOP. It defines the order of operations, required layers, decision gates, artifacts, and governance rules that must be satisfied before AJ Digital moves from problem discovery to production build.

The core sequence is:

```txt
Problem
-> Intelligence
-> Validation
-> Specification
-> Architecture
-> Build
-> Operations
-> Growth
```

Non-negotiable principle:

```txt
Technology is never the starting point.
Operational reality is.
```

## 2. Executive Summary

Most products fail because they begin with a solution narrative before confirming demand, workflow fit, willingness to pay, operating constraints, or distribution. The artifact gets built, but the business problem remains underdiagnosed.

Most AI projects fail because they treat model access as strategy. A model can accelerate reasoning, content, classification, extraction, routing, support, or execution, but it cannot compensate for unclear process ownership, weak data, missing success criteria, poor governance, or absent adoption paths.

Most automation projects fail because they automate unstable workflows. When the underlying process is ambiguous, fragmented, or politically unowned, automation increases speed without increasing clarity. The result is faster leakage, faster confusion, and harder debugging.

Founder-led businesses struggle with implementation because the founder often holds the operating logic in their head. Revenue flow, client context, decision history, lead quality, follow-up rules, delivery standards, and exceptions may exist as tacit knowledge instead of governed system memory.

The Founder Intelligence Product Factory exists to prevent that failure mode. Its role is to force a disciplined progression:

- identify proven demand before committing build resources;
- diagnose founder and business operating reality before prescribing tools;
- convert problems into approved specifications before implementation;
- design architecture before code;
- build through governed human-agent loops;
- operate the system after launch;
- measure adoption, attribution, retention, and ROI before scaling.

## 3. Foundational Doctrine

### 3.1 Problem Before Solution

Every system begins with a clearly defined business problem. A product idea, AI feature, automation, dashboard, or agent is not valid until the problem has been described in operational terms:

- who is affected;
- what breaks or leaks;
- where it appears in the workflow;
- how often it occurs;
- what it costs;
- what changes if it is fixed.

### 3.2 Demand Before Development

Development begins only after demand has credible evidence. Evidence can come from market research, customer interviews, review mining, competitor analysis, acquisition-market signals, internal revenue leakage, support patterns, or observed workflow failure.

Demand is not proven by enthusiasm, trend alignment, founder intuition, or tool availability.

### 3.3 Specification Before Implementation

No complex build begins without a written specification. The specification must define scope, user roles, functional requirements, non-functional requirements, constraints, success metrics, dependencies, and explicit out-of-scope items.

### 3.4 Architecture Before Code

Architecture defines how the system should work before implementation begins. It must address frontend, backend, APIs, database, authentication, authorization, observability, infrastructure, security, scalability, and integration boundaries where applicable.

### 3.5 Operations Before Automation

AJ Digital does not automate broken processes without naming the breakage. A process must be made legible before it is automated. The operating rule is:

```txt
Clarify -> Govern -> Automate -> Monitor -> Improve
```

### 3.6 Evidence Before Assumptions

Assumptions may be documented, but they must not be presented as facts. Every major decision must distinguish:

- known facts;
- inferences;
- assumptions;
- open questions;
- risks;
- required validation.

### 3.7 Anti-Patterns

The following patterns violate this doctrine:

- building before validation;
- tool-first thinking;
- AI-first thinking;
- automating broken or politically unowned processes;
- treating a demo as a deployable operating system;
- premature scaling before adoption and reliability are proven;
- creating dashboards without action ownership;
- writing code before the PRD or architecture is approved;
- confusing model capability with business readiness;
- treating founder memory as documentation;
- adding integrations before the source workflow is stable;
- introducing a new source of truth without retiring or governing the old one.

## 4. Layer 1 - Opportunity Intelligence

### Purpose

Opportunity Intelligence identifies proven demand before product or system commitments are made.

### Coverage

This layer may include:

- market research;
- competitive intelligence;
- Acquire.com analysis;
- TrustMRR analysis;
- G2 review mining;
- Reddit research;
- customer interviews;
- revenue opportunity assessment.

### Required Questions

- What problem is visible in the market?
- Who experiences it?
- What evidence shows urgency or willingness to pay?
- What alternatives already exist?
- Where do alternatives fail?
- What distribution path is realistic?
- What would make this opportunity too small, too expensive, or too fragile?

### Output Artifacts

- Opportunity Brief
- Market Gap Analysis
- Demand Validation Report

### Success Criteria

- Demand evidence is documented.
- Competitor and substitute options are identified.
- Buyer or user segment is specific.
- The opportunity has a plausible acquisition, adoption, or revenue path.
- Risks and disconfirming evidence are included.

### Failure Indicators

- The opportunity depends only on trend interest.
- No buyer, user, or operator is clearly defined.
- Competitors are ignored or misrepresented.
- Distribution is assumed but not explained.
- The business case is based on speculative market size alone.

## 5. Layer 2 - Founder Intelligence

### Purpose

Founder Intelligence identifies operational bottlenecks inside the business before prescribing a system.

### Coverage

This layer may include:

- revenue leaks;
- lead flow analysis;
- follow-up failures;
- attribution gaps;
- communication breakdowns;
- knowledge fragmentation;
- workflow bottlenecks.

### Required Questions

- Where does revenue leak?
- Where do leads stall, decay, or disappear?
- Which follow-up steps are inconsistent?
- Which attribution signals are missing or unreliable?
- Which decisions depend on undocumented founder knowledge?
- Which handoffs create rework or confusion?
- Which reports exist, and which decisions do they actually support?

### Output Artifacts

- Founder Intelligence Diagnostic
- Revenue Leak Assessment
- Operational Intelligence Report

### Success Criteria

- Bottlenecks are mapped to real workflows.
- Revenue, time, quality, or risk impact is estimated.
- Ownership is identified for each failure point.
- Manual workarounds and hidden dependencies are documented.
- The system opportunity is tied to operational reality.

### Failure Indicators

- The diagnosis jumps directly to software.
- Founder assumptions are not tested against workflow evidence.
- Bottlenecks are described generally instead of operationally.
- No owner is named for the future process.
- The proposed system does not address a measurable leak or constraint.

## 6. Layer 3 - Product Intelligence

### Purpose

Product Intelligence converts validated problems into approved specifications.

### Coverage

This layer includes:

- PRDs;
- user stories;
- functional requirements;
- non-functional requirements;
- constraints;
- scope definition;
- success metrics.

### Output Artifacts

- Product Requirements Document
- User Story Map
- Functional Requirements Specification
- Non-Functional Requirements Register
- Scope and Constraint Register
- Success Metrics Definition

### Approval Gates

- Problem statement approved.
- In-scope and out-of-scope boundaries approved.
- User roles and workflows approved.
- Success metrics approved.
- Known risks accepted or assigned.

### Success Criteria

- Requirements are specific enough for architecture work.
- Scope is bounded and testable.
- Success metrics are measurable.
- Dependencies and constraints are visible.
- The operator can identify what will not be built.

### Failure Indicators

- The PRD reads like a feature wish list.
- Scope boundaries are absent.
- Requirements cannot be tested.
- Success criteria are vague.
- Implementation begins before approval.

## 7. Layer 4 - Architecture Intelligence

### Purpose

Architecture Intelligence designs systems before implementation.

### Coverage

This layer may include:

- frontend architecture;
- backend architecture;
- APIs;
- databases;
- authentication;
- authorization;
- observability;
- infrastructure;
- security;
- scalability.

It must also evaluate where applicable:

- API design;
- database selection;
- caching;
- load balancing;
- production infrastructure;
- integration boundaries;
- data ownership;
- failure modes;
- rollback paths.

### Output Artifacts

- Architecture Specification
- System Design Document
- Data Model
- Integration Map
- Security and Access Model
- Observability Plan
- Deployment and Rollback Plan

### Success Criteria

- Components, boundaries, and ownership are clear.
- Data model and integration contracts are defined.
- Authentication and authorization are addressed.
- Observability and failure handling are specified.
- Deployment and rollback paths are known before build.
- Architecture choices are justified against constraints.

### Failure Indicators

- Code begins before system boundaries are approved.
- Data ownership is unclear.
- Authentication or authorization is deferred without a written reason.
- Observability is treated as post-launch cleanup.
- The system depends on unmanaged manual steps.
- A vendor or platform becomes canonical without review.

## 8. Layer 5 - Build Intelligence

### Purpose

Build Intelligence transforms approved specifications and architecture into working systems through governed human-agent execution.

### Coverage

Build work may involve:

- Claude Code;
- Codex;
- Cursor;
- Copilot;
- OpenClaw;
- Hermes.

### Required Operating Controls

- Human approval gates before serious or irreversible changes.
- Review cycles for code, copy, architecture, and data behavior.
- Validation loops for tests, typechecks, builds, linting, runtime checks, and manual QA where relevant.
- Explicit separation between planning, implementation, review, and deployment.
- No secret exposure.
- No silent overwrite of doctrine, naming, architecture, or source-of-truth files.

### Output Artifacts

- Implementation Plan
- Pull Request or Patch Set
- Test Evidence
- Review Notes
- Deployment Readiness Notes
- Session Closeout

### Success Criteria

- The build traces back to an approved PRD and architecture.
- Tests or validation commands are documented.
- Review findings are addressed or explicitly accepted.
- The implementation is reversible where practical.
- The operator knows what changed, what remains open, and what is safe to deploy.

### Failure Indicators

- The agent builds from a vague prompt.
- Code changes are not tied to requirements.
- Tests are skipped without disclosure.
- Production deploy is implied by local success.
- Secrets, client data, or protected systems are touched without approval.

## 9. Layer 6 - Operational Intelligence

### Purpose

Operational Intelligence governs the system after launch so it becomes part of business operations instead of an abandoned artifact.

### Coverage

This layer may include:

- CRM;
- communications;
- reporting;
- dashboards;
- Business Memory;
- documentation;
- monitoring;
- workflow governance.

### Output Artifacts

- Operating Playbook
- Support and Escalation Map
- Reporting Dashboard
- Business Memory Schema
- Monitoring Plan
- Workflow Governance Register
- Documentation Index

### Success Criteria

- Operators know how to use, monitor, and maintain the system.
- Reports map to decisions and owners.
- Alerts have response paths.
- Documentation is discoverable and current enough for handoff.
- Business Memory captures decisions, exceptions, and recurring patterns.

### Failure Indicators

- Launch occurs without ownership.
- Dashboards exist without decision workflows.
- Alerts have no triage path.
- Documentation is scattered or stale at launch.
- Business-critical context remains only in a founder's head.

## 10. Layer 7 - Growth Intelligence

### Purpose

Growth Intelligence improves and expands systems after operational fit is proven.

### Coverage

This layer may include:

- adoption;
- customer success;
- retention;
- expansion opportunities;
- attribution;
- ROI measurement.

### Output Artifacts

- Adoption Report
- Customer Success Feedback Summary
- Retention and Expansion Analysis
- Attribution Report
- ROI Review
- Growth Backlog

### Success Criteria

- Adoption is measured by actual use, not launch completion.
- Retention or repeat-use patterns are visible.
- Expansion opportunities are linked to evidence.
- Attribution connects system activity to business outcomes where possible.
- Growth work is prioritized by impact, risk, and operational capacity.

### Failure Indicators

- Scaling begins before adoption is proven.
- Customer success feedback is absent.
- ROI is asserted without measurement.
- Attribution remains unclear.
- Expansion work creates new operational complexity without governance.

## 11. Decision Gates

No layer may progress without passing the previous gate. Gates may be revisited when new evidence changes the decision basis.

| Gate | Required Inputs | Review Criteria | Failure Conditions | Required Approvals |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Opportunity Validation Gate | Opportunity Brief, Market Gap Analysis, Demand Validation Report | Demand evidence, buyer clarity, competitor awareness, distribution plausibility, disconfirming evidence | No clear buyer, weak demand evidence, ignored alternatives, speculative distribution | Founder/operator approval |
| Founder Diagnostic Gate | Founder Intelligence Diagnostic, Revenue Leak Assessment, Operational Intelligence Report | Workflow reality, revenue or time impact, bottleneck ownership, hidden dependencies | Tool-first recommendation, vague bottlenecks, no process owner, no measurable operational issue | Founder/operator approval |
| PRD Approval Gate | PRD, user stories, requirements, constraints, success metrics | Scope clarity, testability, success metrics, out-of-scope boundaries, dependency visibility | Wish-list PRD, no measurable criteria, unclear users, unresolved blocking questions | Product owner and founder/operator approval |
| Architecture Approval Gate | Architecture Specification, System Design Document, Data Model, Integration Map | Boundary clarity, security, data ownership, deployment path, rollback path, observability | Deferred core security, unclear data model, unmanaged integrations, no rollback plan | Technical owner and founder/operator approval |
| Build Approval Gate | Implementation Plan, approved spec, approved architecture | Change scope, risk level, validation plan, reversibility, agent dispatch clarity | Agent begins from vague prompt, no validation plan, destructive change without approval | Founder/operator approval using `proceed` for serious changes |
| Operational Readiness Gate | Test evidence, review notes, operating playbook, monitoring plan, deployment notes | Supportability, monitoring, documentation, ownership, escalation path | Local-only validation presented as production readiness, no owner, no monitoring, no rollback | Technical owner and operator approval |
| Growth Review Gate | Adoption Report, Attribution Report, ROI Review, Growth Backlog | Real usage, retention signals, attribution confidence, operational capacity | Scaling without adoption, unmeasured ROI, unclear attribution, unmanaged complexity | Founder/operator approval |

## 15. Governance Rules

1. Do not begin with software. Begin with operational reality.
2. Do not build before demand and workflow evidence are documented.
3. Do not automate before the process is clear enough to govern.
4. Do not implement before the PRD and architecture are approved.
5. Do not scale before adoption, attribution, retention, and operational capacity are evaluated.
6. Do not give multiple AI assistants the same broad task. Dispatch bounded work.
7. Do not treat local validation as production readiness.
8. Do not create duplicate source-of-truth documents.
9. Do not make a vendor, framework, or tool canonical without architecture review.
10. Do not bypass human approval gates for serious or irreversible work.
